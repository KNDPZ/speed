How it measures

Sources run one at a time so they never fight for bandwidth: 5 pings (jitter comes from their variation), then 6 s of parallel streamed downloads. Upload runs against Cloudflare — public CDNs are download-only, so those show —.

Server IPs are resolved live over DNS-over-HTTPS so you can see exactly which edge you reached. Fast.com, Ookla, TestMy, SpeedOf.Me, nPerf, Speedcheck and BandwidthPlace can't be measured from another page — their servers reject cross-site requests (CORS), so they live in the hub below instead.

Quality grades use Ookla-style thresholds on your latency, jitter, download and upload.

The test hub — cross-check with the big names

These services only run on their own pages (they block embedding and cross-site measurement), so Crossbench launches each in its own tab with one click. Run two or three and compare against the numbers above — agreement across independent testers is the most trustworthy result you can get.

Internet speed test FAQ

What is a good internet speed?

For everyday browsing, 10–25 Mbps download is comfortable. HD streaming needs about 10 Mbps per screen and 4K streaming needs 25 Mbps or more. Online gaming cares less about bandwidth and more about a ping under 60 ms with low jitter. For video calls, aim for at least 3 Mbps upload speed.

Why test against multiple servers?

A single-server speed test only tells you how fast you reach that one server. This WiFi and broadband speed test measures your bandwidth against nine independent networks, so you can see how your ISP routes to different parts of the internet — that spread explains why Netflix can stutter while downloads fly, or vice versa.

What do ping and jitter mean?

Ping (latency) is how long a round trip to the server takes, in milliseconds — lower is better. Jitter is how much that time varies between packets; high jitter causes choppy calls and rubber-banding in games even when your download speed test looks fast.

Why is my speed lower than what my ISP advertises?

WiFi interference, old routers, other devices streaming, VPNs, and peak-hour congestion all reduce real-world throughput. Test over an ethernet cable, close other apps, and run this bandwidth check at different times of day. If wired speeds are consistently far below your plan, share your result link with your ISP as evidence.
